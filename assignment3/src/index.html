Bald eagle

The Bald eagle's The head, neck, and tail of bald eagles are bright, plain white, but younger birds may show spotting. Their eyes, bill, legs, and feet are yellow, and their black talons are thick and powerful.

Description

Although the head of the bald eagle seems to be bald, it is really covered with white feathers. Its name really derives from a more ancient word that meant "white-headed." The mature bald eagles' "bald" heads stand out vividly against their chocolate brown bodies. They have a very broad, thick, yellow bill with a highly hooked upper mandible. The average length of the bird is 35 to 42 inches, and its wingspan can reach 7 feet or more.

Bald eagles pluck fish out of the water with their talons, and sometimes they follow seabirds as a means of locating fish. Bald eagles also rob ospreys of their fish. Besides live fish, bald eagles also prey on other birds, small mammals, snakes, turtles, and crabs, and they readily eat carrion.

Breeding

The whole continental United States and much of Canada are included in the bald eagle's range. They may be found in a variety of settings, including the New England deciduous woodlands, Californian deserts, and Louisianan bayous. It is the only endemic (native) sea eagle in North America. Bald eagles mate from late September to early April, depending on the region. The female lays her first egg five to 10 days after mating and incubates the eggs for about 35 days. They produce one to three eggs, which is called the clutch size.

Bald eagle chicks are born with fluffy white down covering them, but they swiftly grow larger and acquire adult feathers. The distinguishing white head and tail of juvenile birds do not appear until they are 4 to 5 years old, when they are sexually mature and able to mate. Juvenile birds have mottled brown and white plumage.

Poaching, accidental or intentional shootings, as well as other dangers to raptors including pollution, run-ins with wind turbines or power lines, tainted food sources, and habitat degradation all pose threats to bald eagles today. A serious concern to bald eagles and other big raptors is lead poisoning from fishing lures and used ammunition. `

Life span

Bald Eagles can live a long time. The oldest recorded bird in the wild was at least 38 years old when it was hit and killed by a car in New York in 2015. It had been banded in the same state in 1977.

Bald eagles have an unusually long lifespan compared to other birds. This is because they are top predators who don't have to worry about becoming prey to other animals. However, the bald eagle's lifespan comes up short compared to other eagles. On average, other well-known eagle species, such as the harpy eagle, have been estimated to live anywhere between 25-35 years in the wild. Conservation

The bald eagle's population is growing, according to the International Union for Conservation of Nature, which rates its conservation as being of "least concern." However, chemicals, particularly DDT, which was widely used after World War II, had a significant negative impact on bald eagle populations. According to the California Department of Fish and Wildlife, the once-touted herbicide poisoned bald eagles and caused their eggshells to grow thin, leading to several unsuccessful nesting attempts.

The bald eagle was listed as an endangered species by the federal government in 1967 and by California in 1971 due to its declining populations. Strong attempts to recover these birds were effective when the use of DDT was outlawed in the United States in 1972, and in 2007 the bald eagle was taken off the list of endangered species.
